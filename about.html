Background

Grant Crossman brings over a decade of classroom teaching experience across British, American, and IB environments in the UAE — a professional background that shapes every aspect of how Open Horizons operates. Unlike tutors who move directly from school to private practice, Grant's teaching career spans the full range of international curricula, giving him an unusually clear picture of how different schools approach learning and where students commonly fall behind.

His core subjects — Mathematics, Science, and English — reflect both depth of expertise and the reality of where students most often need structured support. Evidence-Based Practice

The research that shapes every session

Open Horizons teaching is grounded in three well-established bodies of educational research. These are not marketing terms — they are the frameworks that directly inform how Grant plans, delivers, and adjusts instruction for each student.

01

Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction

Barak Rosenshine

A set of ten evidence-backed principles distilled from decades of classroom research, describing how expert teachers introduce content, check for understanding, and build student fluency. These principles guide the pace and structure of every tutoring session — from the amount of new material introduced at once to the use of frequent low-stakes checks throughout.

Applied in sessions as Structured review, guided practice, high success-rate tasks, and scaffolded independent work — adjusted to each student's current level of mastery.
02

Cognitive Load Theory

John Sweller

A theory of how the working memory processes and retains new information. When instruction is poorly designed — too much presented at once, unclear sequencing, no prior knowledge activated — students struggle not because they lack ability, but because the lesson itself is placing too much demand on their cognitive resources. Awareness of cognitive load shapes how content is broken down, sequenced, and explained.

Applied in sessions as Worked examples, stepwise reasoning, visual strategies, and careful management of the volume of new material introduced in any single session.
03

Hattie's Visible Learning

John Hattie

A synthesis of over 800 meta-analyses covering what factors most reliably improve student outcomes. Hattie's research identifies the highest-impact teaching strategies — including formative feedback, teacher-student relationships, and metacognitive skill development — and separates them from popular practices that show little measurable effect.

Applied in sessions as Deliberate feedback loops, transparent learning goals, confidence tracking, and regular progress reviews communicated clearly to students and parents.
How It Works

Three principles.
One consistent approach.

Every student engagement follows the same structured methodology — regardless of subject, year group, or curriculum. The sequence is not arbitrary. It reflects what the research consistently shows about how learning actually sticks.

The result is a programme that is genuinely personalised — not just in tone, but in content, pacing, and the specific gaps being addressed.

See the Full Approach
01
Diagnostic Precision
Before any instruction begins, structured diagnostic assessment identifies conceptual gaps, procedural misunderstandings, and curriculum misalignments specific to each student. Teaching starts from the right place — not an assumed one.
02
Conceptual Clarity
Instruction prioritises understanding before procedure, reasoning before memorisation. In Mathematics and Science this means conceptual modelling and stepwise reasoning. In English it means writing structure, clarity, and analytical reading — not just surface corrections.
03
Personalised Growth
Progress is tracked against individual goals. Parents receive structured updates. Students are taught metacognitive strategies — retrieval practice, spaced repetition, exam planning — so that progress extends beyond the sessions themselves.
